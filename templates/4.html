Moko Disease Fact Sheet


What is Moko?

  • Moko is a severe bacterial disease caused by Ralstonia solanacearum race 2, which also causes bugtok of banana and is closely related to the bacterium responsible for blood disease.
  • Moko is a major cause of banana crop losses, particularly in Central and South America, the Caribbean, and the Philippines, where it can reduce yields by up to 70% in some areas.

What are the Symptoms?

  • Leaf symptoms: Yellowing and wilting of leaves, with younger leaves developing pale green or whitish patches before dying. Suckers may also wilt.
  • Stem symptoms: Internal discoloration of the stem, starting as cream or yellow and progressing to brown or black. Bacterial ooze is often present when the stem is cut.
  • Fruit symptoms: Premature ripening of some fingers in otherwise green bunches, internal discoloration (yellow, then brown or grey), and firm rot of the fruit.
  • Infected flower buds and peduncles may become blackened and shriveled.

What can Moko be confused with?

  • Moko is often mistaken for blood disease, bugtok, or Panama disease.
  • Key differences: Panama disease does not cause internal fruit discoloration, bacterial ooze, or premature ripening, while bugtok does not cause wilting of the entire plant.
  • Laboratory testing is required to distinguish between Moko, blood disease, and other diseases.

How is Moko Spread?

  • Moko bacteria can spread via infected plant parts, including roots, stems, suckers, and fruit. Root-to-root infection can also occur.
  • The bacteria can persist in the soil for over 18 months, spreading through contaminated tools, machinery, shoes, hands, and even water run-off.
  • Insects can spread Moko from infected male buds to other flowers, and infected fruit shipments are another potential source of infection.

Where is Moko Found?

  • Moko is endemic to Central and South America, the Caribbean, Jamaica, the Philippines, and Guam.
  • It was detected in Cairns, Australia in 1989 on imported heliconia plants, but the outbreak was successfully eradicated.

Prevention and Mitigation Strategies

  • Pest-Free Planting Material: Always use certified pest-free planting material, including tissue-cultured plants, to avoid introducing Moko into new areas.
  • Farm Hygiene: Implement strict farm hygiene practices by regularly sanitizing tools, machinery, and equipment. Avoid the movement of soil or plant material between infected and healthy areas.
  • Monitoring and Early Detection: Frequently inspect crops for unusual symptoms, especially yellowing or wilting leaves, and report any cases of bacterial ooze or sudden plant death.
  • Removal of Infected Plants: Promptly uproot and destroy infected plants, including their rhizomes and roots, to prevent the bacteria from spreading to nearby plants.
  • Insect Control: Control insects that can spread Moko by using traps, insecticides, and biological control agents such as beneficial predators or parasites.
  • Soil Management: Avoid replanting banana crops in infected soils for at least 18 months to prevent the spread of the bacteria.

How to Protect Your Farm?

  • Use only pest-free planting material and regularly monitor your farm for any unusual symptoms.
